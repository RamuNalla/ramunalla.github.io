Why sparse MoE routers use temperature schedules over the gate softmax: router collapse, exploration, and staying aligned with hard top-k dispatch.

If you have worked with a sparse Mixture-of-Experts block, you already know the rhythm: a router scores every expert for each token, you take top-k, and only those feed-forward experts actually run. Under the hood there is still a softmax story—usually a full distribution when you need importance or an auxiliary loss, and a smaller softmax over just the k chosen logits when you mix their outputs.

Temperature annealing is the idea that you should not treat that softmax as fixed forever. You divide logits by a temperature T before the exponential, and you let T change over training. Early on you often want a softer gate so mass does not pile onto one expert by accident; later you want something closer to the sharp, discrete routing you will actually evaluate. Nothing here replaces load balancing or good initialization, but temperature is a simple knob on how “spread out” the router feels week to week.

The rest of this post stays in that lane:

  • Why that knob matters for MoE routing
  • What temperature does to the gate, in plain terms
  • What people usually mean by a schedule
  • Where temperature sits next to top-k and auxiliary losses
  • A few ways things go wrong when the pieces do not line up

1. Why bother with temperature for routing?

A few failure modes show up often enough that it helps to name them:

  • Router collapse — the gate peaks on a handful of experts almost immediately; top-k keeps sending traffic there, other experts rarely see gradients through their FFNs, and you paid for a wide layer that only trains like a narrow one.
  • A gate that stays too soft — if T stays large forever, specialization can feel mushy compared to the hard top-k path you actually execute, unless you trained with that mismatch in mind.
  • Train vs eval skew — auxiliary loss uses one temperature and top-k uses another; at eval the router no longer matches what training seemed to optimize.

Annealing is one response to the first two. Roughly:

  • Start with T above one so the full softmax spreads mass and more experts get meaningful probability under the metrics you log or regularize.
  • Drift T down toward one (or whatever you use at evaluation) so the gate moves toward peaked behavior that matches hard routing and stable expert roles.
  • You often get a gentler optimization landscape early—less winner-take-all while the router and experts are still moving fast.

I still treat auxiliary load balancing and sensible hyperparameters as the main line of defense. Temperature is an extra lever on the shape of the gate over time, not a substitute for them.

2. What temperature does to the gate

Write router logits as z over E experts and pick a positive temperature T. The familiar softmax is p_i proportional to exp(z_i / T). In words:

  • T > 1 — logits are scaled down before exp, so the distribution softens and more experts share mass.
  • T = 1 — ordinary softmax; the usual reference point.
  • T < 1 — the distribution sharpens; largest logits dominate more (winner-take-all among experts).

In an actual system, two questions get answered differently:

  • Full softmax over E experts — where you need the whole vector (importance, auxiliary losses); temperature lives here when you scale logits before exp.
  • Top-k dispatch — which experts actually run; logits may be raw or scaled—pick one story and use it consistently between train and eval.

Top-k answers “which experts run”; the full softmax answers “how the gate distributes belief.” Those two should be wired so you can explain them in one sentence to your future self.

3. Schedules in plain language

Annealing just means T is not constant—it depends on step or epoch. A common pattern is to warm up with T_start above one so the early phase does not collapse the gate, then decay toward T_end (often one) so you finish near what you will measure at eval.

People use different shapes for that decay:

  • Linear — T steps from T_start to T_end over N optimizer steps.
  • Cosine or smooth curves — same intent, fewer kinks in the schedule.
  • Plateau then drop — hold T > 1 for a fixed window, then step or ramp down.

Plenty of stacks never touch T and instead rely on load-balancing loss, noise, or capacity tweaks alone. That is fine. Temperature is optional; it helps to know what a recipe means when it mentions T, and to treat it as one knob among several.

4. Where this lands in the pipeline

Per token and per MoE layer, the router emits logits g. In practice the pieces stack like this:

  • Full distribution — for monitoring or auxiliary terms, you may use g_scaled = g / T before softmax over all E experts.
  • Dispatch — top-k on the logits your codebase has chosen (raw or scaled); training and eval should agree.
  • Mixture weights — often a softmax over only the k selected logits; some teams temperature-scale that step too.

None of this turns sparse MoE into “run all experts softly for cheap.” You still execute k experts per token unless you deliberately build a dense mixture.

5. Where things go sideways

The frustrating bugs are usually inconsistency:

  • Mismatched aux loss — load balancing uses softmax at T = 1 on g, but another branch uses g / T; you are no longer optimizing quite the same statistics you think you are.
  • T decays too fast — the gate sharpens mid-training and collapse can reappear.
  • T stays high too long — training stays softer than the hard top-k behavior you care about; experts may not specialize as cleanly.

Temperature also does not remove the need for a real load-balancing term, sensible k and expert count, and a stable setup for the full model. It is easy to add; making the whole MoE block behave is still the larger job.

6. A small code sketch

Here is the one-liner people mean when they say “temperature on the gate softmax”:

import torch.nn.functional as F

def gate_softmax_with_temperature(
    gate_logits: torch.Tensor, temperature: float
) -> torch.Tensor:
    return F.softmax(gate_logits / temperature, dim=-1)

When you wire this in:

  • Pass temperature from something like schedule(step) in the training loop.
  • Keep top-k as its own step—often torch.topk(...) on the same logits policy you use at evaluation.
  • Match that policy to whatever the auxiliary loss uses so the gate does not train on one story and deploy on another.

Conclusion

Temperature annealing in MoE is really about scheduling how peaked the router’s distribution is over experts: room to explore early, then sharper behavior that matches top-k routing and stable roles. It works best when the story is coherent—one rule for the full softmax, one for top-k, and auxiliary losses that see the same scaling the router trains on.

If you walk away with a short checklist:

  • Anneal T on purpose and document the schedule.
  • Be explicit where T applies (full softmax vs logits fed to top-k).
  • Do not expect temperature to replace load balancing or the rest of MoE engineering—it is a lever, not the whole machine.
