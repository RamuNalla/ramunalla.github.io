Standard language model inference is often described as “System 1” thinking—fast, instinctive, and reactive. When you ask a vanilla GPT-4 or Llama-3 model a question, it begins predicting the answer token-by-token in a single forward pass. While this works for creative writing, it fails catastrophically for multi-step logic.

The industry has moved toward Chain-of-Thought (CoT) and its successor, Inference-Time Compute Scaling, to elicit “System 2” thinking—deliberative, slow, and logical. By allowing a model to “think” before it “speaks,” we unlock reasoning capabilities that are fundamentally inaccessible to models restricted to direct answering.

In this post, I will explore the origins of CoT, the mathematical justification for why it works, the move toward Majority Voting, and the new frontier of production efficiency: Chain-of-Draft (CoD).

1. The Origins

Chain-of-Thought didn’t start as a modeling breakthrough; it started as a discovery in prompt engineering.

The Google Brain Revelation (2022)

In early 2022, researchers at Google Brain published “Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning in Large Language Models.” They found that Large Language Models (LLMs) were failing simple arithmetic not because they lacked the “knowledge,” but because they lacked the working memory.

By providing a few examples where the solution was explained step-by-step (Few-Shot CoT), the model’s accuracy on the GSM8K benchmark jumped from mediocre to state-of-the-art.

Zero-Shot CoT: The “Magic” of Deliberation

Shortly after, researchers discovered that you didn’t even need examples. By simply appending the string “Let’s think step by step” to a prompt, the model would self-induce a reasoning trajectory.

Example:

  • Direct Prompt: “If John has 5 apples and eats 2, then buys 3 more, how many does he have?” -> Answer: 6.
  • CoT Prompt: “Let’s think step by step.” -> Answer: John starts with 5. He eats 2 (5-2=3). He buys 3 (3+3=6). Final answer: 6.

2. The Modeling Pivot: Distilling the Habit

Today, we no longer rely on prompting tricks. We now distill reasoning into models through Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and Reinforcement Learning (RL).

Why CoT Works: The Token-as-Compute Theory

Why does writing out steps make a model smarter? It comes down to the Transformer architecture. A Transformer has a fixed amount of computation (FLOPs) it can perform per token generated.

If a model answers “6” immediately, it has exactly one forward pass to calculate the entire logic of the problem. If the model writes 50 tokens of reasoning, it has performed 50 forward passes before committing to the final answer. Chain-of-Thought is essentially a way to scale the “depth” of the model’s processing through sequence length.

The Evolution into “o1” and DeepSeek-R1

The modern era (2025-2026) has moved beyond Distillation toward RL-based Reasoning. Models like OpenAI’s o1 and DeepSeek-R1 are trained with rewards for correct answers, allowing them to discover their own reasoning strategies—including backtracking, self-correction, and “double-checking.”

<thought> 
Step 1: Calculate initial state.
Step 2: Apply first transformation.
[reflection] Wait, did I account for the tax? Let me re-calculate...
Step 3: Apply second transformation.
</thought>
<answer> Final Result </answer>

3. Scaling Compute During Inference

The most significant shift in LLM research is the Inference Scaling Law. We have learned that if we give a model more “time to think” at inference time, its intelligence increases even without further training.

Majority Voting (Self-Consistency)

One of the most robust ways to use CoT today is Self-Consistency (Majority CoT). Instead of generating one reasoning path, we generate \(N\) paths (e.g., \(N=5\)).

  1. Path A: … logic … Answer: 42
  2. Path B: … logic … Answer: 42
  3. Path C: … logic … Answer: 40
  4. Path D: … logic … Answer: 42
  5. Path E: … logic … Answer: 40

By taking the Majority Vote (42), we filter out “stochastic errors”—instances where the model understood the logic but made a minor calculation slip in a single path.

Pass@N and Verifiers

In high-stakes environments, we use a Verifier model (a Reward Model) to score the logical consistency of each path. This allows us to scale “test-time compute” to solve problems that a single-pass model would find impossible.

4. Chain-of-Draft (CoD): The Production Frontier

While CoT is powerful, it is expensive. In production, paying for 200 “thinking tokens” for every query is unsustainable. This led to the development of Chain-of-Draft (CoD).

The Concept: Minimalist Reasoning

CoD is based on the idea that once a model is “smart” enough, it doesn’t need to explain its logic in full natural language sentences. It can use a shorthand “draft” notation.

  • Full CoT: “First, I will take the 50 units and find 20 percent. 20 percent of 50 is 10. Then I subtract 10 from 50 to get 40…”
  • Chain-of-Draft: “50*0.2=10 -> 50-10=40 -> 40+15=55”

Technical Justification for CoD

CoD distillation aims to maintain the computational benefits of CoT (using multiple tokens to “think”) while stripping away the linguistic overhead.

Feature Full Chain-of-Thought Chain-of-Draft
Token Usage High (100-500 tokens) Low (15-30 tokens)
Latency 5s - 20s 0.5s - 2s
Logic Fidelity High High (if distilled correctly)
Human Readability High Low (Symbolic)

By distilling a large model’s full CoT into a small model’s CoD, we achieve a “System 2” capability that is fast enough for edge deployment and real-time agents.

Conclusion: The Path Forward

The future of LLM reasoning is not about bigger models, but about better thinking. Chain-of-Thought has evolved from a simple “Let’s think step by step” prompt into a sophisticated modeling framework where we scale compute at the moment of inference.

Whether through Majority Voting for high-accuracy research or Chain-of-Draft for low-latency production, the ability to orchestrate a model’s internal monologue is the defining skill of the modern AI engineer. We are no longer just training models to “know”; we are training them to “deliberate.”
